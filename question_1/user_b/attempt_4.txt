About eighteen months ago I was coordinating a product launch with VP-level visibility and a six-week runway. The team included two engineers, a UX designer, a QA lead, and a marketing manager — none of them reported to me. One of the senior engineers had delivered their modules two to three days late across four consecutive sprints. When I tracked the downstream effects, those slips were blocking dependent tasks and consuming our QA buffer. We were quietly losing ground.

I decided to address it directly rather than escalate. I built a dependency map — each delayed deliverable, the downstream tasks it blocked, and the total buffer days burned — and used it as the starting point for a conversation, not a complaint.

I asked the engineer if we could grab coffee, keeping it casual. I led with a question: how were things going? They told me they were being assigned to P1 support incidents by another team lead with no advance notice. That reframed everything.

I shared the dependency map as shared context, then asked: "What would make it easier to protect your time for this project?" They drove the solution — blocking mornings for focused project work and flagging risks to me before they became delays. I absorbed two lower-priority deliverables by redistributing them to a team member with capacity.

Their delivery improved within the first week. When one external incident came up midway through, they flagged it two days early. We adjusted and launched on time.

At the retrospective, they said the conversation had made them feel like a partner rather than a problem. That was the outcome that mattered most.
