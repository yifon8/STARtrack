About eighteen months ago I was the project coordinator for a product launch with VP-level visibility and a six-week runway. The cross-functional team included two engineers, a UX designer, a QA lead, and a marketing manager — none of them reported to me.

One of the engineers, a senior on their core team, had delivered modules two to three days late across four consecutive sprints. When I tracked the cascade, those delays were consuming our testing buffer and pushing back the design review and marketing build-out. We were quietly losing ground.

I needed to change the pattern without formal authority and without damaging the collaboration. My instinct was to avoid escalating to their manager. But I'll be honest: partway through, I started questioning that instinct — maybe I should have involved a manager earlier. I still think the path I chose was right, but I held onto some uncertainty about it even after the project wrapped.

I built a dependency map — each late deliverable, the downstream tasks it blocked, and the buffer days we'd burned. I wanted to lead with data rather than frustration.

I asked the engineer if we could grab coffee, keeping the invite casual. My plan was to listen before introducing my concerns. When I asked how things were going, they told me they were being assigned to P1 support incidents by a different team lead with no advance notice. They were genuinely trying to hold both responsibilities and losing ground on both.

That changed my framing. I shared the dependency map as shared context, then asked: "What would make it easier to protect time for this project?" They drove most of the solution: blocking 9am–12pm for focused project work and sending me a Slack message by EOD if a deliverable was at risk. On my end, I absorbed two lower-priority deliverables and set up a brief weekly 15-minute check-in.

Delivery consistency improved within the first week. When one external incident arose midway through, they flagged it two days early and we adjusted without downstream impact. We launched on time.

The outcome I'm most confident about is also the hardest to measure: the engineer said the conversation made them feel respected rather than managed. I'm less confident about my own timing — I still wonder whether I waited too long to act.
