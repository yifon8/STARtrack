About eighteen months ago I was the project coordinator for a product launch with VP-level visibility and a six-week runway. The cross-functional team included two engineers, a UX designer, a QA lead, and a marketing manager — none of them reported to me.

One of the senior engineers had delivered every module two to three days late across four consecutive sprints. I tracked the cascade: those delays were consuming our QA buffer, compressing the design review, and pushing back the marketing build-out. We were losing ground.

Before starting any conversation, I built a dependency map — each late deliverable, the downstream tasks it blocked, and the total buffer days consumed. I wanted to lead with shared data, not frustration.

I asked the engineer if we could grab coffee, keeping the invite casual. I opened with a question: how were things going? They told me they were being pulled into P1 support incidents by another team lead, mid-sprint, with no advance notice and no capacity buffer. They were managing two high-demand roles simultaneously.

I shared the dependency map as context we both needed, then asked: "What would make it easier to protect your time for this project?" They drove most of the solution — blocking 9am to noon for focused project work and sending me a Slack message by end of day if anything was at risk. I redistributed two of their lower-priority deliverables to a teammate with capacity and set up a 15-minute weekly check-in.

Their delivery improved within a week. When an external incident came up three weeks later, they flagged it two days ahead. We adjusted and launched on time.

At the retrospective, they said unprompted that I had come in curious rather than critical — and that it made them want to come through for the team. That feedback told me more about effective influence than any project tracker could.
