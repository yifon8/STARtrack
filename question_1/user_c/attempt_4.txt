About eighteen months ago I was coordinating a product launch — I think that's roughly when it was. The team was cross-functional: two engineers, a designer, a QA lead, and a marketing manager, though none of them reported to me. One of the senior engineers had been missing deliverables by two to three days across maybe three or four sprints. The cumulative effect was eating into our QA buffer, I think, and putting the launch date at some risk.

I wasn't sure escalating to management was the right move, so I tried to handle it directly — though I'll admit I wasn't fully confident that was the right call either.

I put together a dependency map of sorts, showing which tasks had been blocked by the delays and for how long. I tried to make it neutral rather than accusatory, though I wasn't always sure it came across that way.

I asked the engineer for an informal coffee and led with questions about how things were going. It turned out they were being pulled into P1 support incidents by another team, with no advance warning. That helped explain things, though I'm not sure I knew right away how to respond to it.

We landed on a couple of agreements: they'd block their mornings for project work and flag anything slipping before end of day. I also moved two lower-priority items off their plate by reassigning them to someone else.

Their delivery did improve after that, and we launched on time. I think the approach was probably right — though looking back, I'm not entirely sure I handled the early framing as well as I could have.
