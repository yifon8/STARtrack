
Attempt 5 — Full, Confident Delivery with Nuance (~4.5 minutes spoken)

Situation:
About two years ago, I was the project coordinator for a major product launch — one that had visibility up to the VP level. The team was cross-functional: two engineers, a UX designer, a QA lead, and a marketing manager. None of them reported to me. My role was essentially to hold the whole effort together without any formal power to direct anyone's work.

Around the six-week mark before launch, I started noticing a pattern. One of the senior engineers — someone highly skilled and well-regarded on their core team — was delivering their pieces two to three days late, consistently. At first it felt minor. But when I tracked the downstream effects, I could see that each slip was eating directly into our testing window, which was already tight, and cascading delays into the design review and the marketing build-out. We were quietly falling behind.

Task:
My challenge was clear: I needed to change this person's behavior and protect the project timeline — but without any authority to direct them, and without damaging what was otherwise a productive working relationship. Escalating to their manager was an option, but I considered it a last resort. I've seen that approach breed resentment, create a defensive posture, and ultimately slow things down more. I wanted to try a different path first.

Action:
I spent about an hour before doing anything else. I pulled together a simple visual — a timeline showing the original plan alongside the current actual state, with annotations on which tasks were blocked and for how long. I didn't frame it as an accusation. I framed it as shared data.

Then I sent a message asking if we could grab coffee — just a casual check-in, nothing formal. I deliberately kept the calendar invite vague: "Wanted to sync on the project." My goal in the first few minutes of the conversation was not to present my concerns — it was to listen.

And I'm glad I did. Once I asked how things were going on their end, they opened up. It turned out they were getting pulled into high-priority support incidents by another team, mid-sprint, with no notice. They were genuinely trying to keep up with both streams but were losing ground. They weren't being careless — they were overloaded.

That changed everything about how I approached the rest of the conversation. Instead of presenting the delay data as a problem they'd caused, I reframed it: "Here's the impact on the shared timeline — what would help you protect time for this project?" That shift — from accusation to problem-solving — changed the energy in the room immediately.

We worked through it together. They proposed blocking their mornings for focused project work and giving me a heads-up by end of day if anything was in danger of slipping, rather than letting it surface at the deadline. I committed to working around their protected hours and to absorbing two of their lower-priority deliverables by redistributing them to another team member who had some capacity.

We also agreed on a lightweight weekly check-in — fifteen minutes, just the two of us — so small issues could surface before they became blockers.

Result:
The change was almost immediate. Over the next four weeks, they hit every milestone. When there was one external interruption that threatened a deliverable, they flagged it two days early and we adjusted without crisis. We launched on schedule.

But honestly, what stuck with me wasn't just that we made the deadline. It was what happened at the retrospective after the project closed. This engineer — unprompted — said that the conversation I'd initiated was one of the better ones they'd had on a cross-team project. That I'd come in curious rather than critical, and that it made them want to come through for the team. That kind of feedback tells you something about the approach that a project tracker never could.

What I took away:
Influencing without authority is really about earning it. You earn it by understanding someone's reality before you try to change their behavior. You earn it by bringing data without blame. And you earn it by making it easier for people to succeed, not just holding them accountable for falling short. Those are the conditions under which people choose to follow your lead — and in my experience, that kind of buy-in is more durable than anything a title could compel.
