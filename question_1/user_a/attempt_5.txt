About two years ago, I was the project coordinator for a high-visibility product launch with VP-level visibility. The team was cross-functional: two engineers, a UX designer, a QA lead, and a marketing manager — none of whom reported to me. Six weeks before launch, one of the senior engineers was delivering their pieces two to three days late, consistently. When I tracked the downstream effects, each slip was eating into our testing window and cascading into the design review and marketing build-out. We were quietly falling behind.

I needed to change the pattern and protect the timeline — without any authority to direct this person's work, and without damaging the relationship. Escalating to their manager felt like a last resort, so I wanted to try a different path first.

Before doing anything else, I built a simple dependency map — original plan alongside current state, with annotations on which tasks were blocked and for how long. I framed it as shared data, not an accusation.

Then I sent a message asking if we could grab coffee — casual, nothing formal. My goal for the first part of the conversation was to listen. When I asked how things were going, they told me they were being pulled into high-priority support incidents by another team, mid-sprint, with no notice. They weren't being careless — they were overloaded.

That changed everything. Instead of presenting the delays as a problem they'd caused, I reframed it: "Here's the impact on our shared timeline — what would help you protect time for this project?" They proposed blocking their mornings for focused project work and flagging anything at risk by end of day. I committed to absorbing two lower-priority deliverables by redistributing them to a team member with capacity, and set up a brief weekly check-in so blockers could surface before they became delays.

Over the next four weeks, they hit every milestone. When one external interruption came up, they flagged it two days early and we adjusted without any downstream impact. We launched on schedule.

At the retrospective, the engineer said unprompted that I'd come in curious rather than critical — and that it made them want to come through for the team. That feedback told me more about the approach than any project tracker could.
